About This Project

According to a 2025 article published by ‘The Guardian’, human connection to nature has declined by 60% in the last 200 years.

Despite Britain having no shortage of woodlands, parks, public gardens and natural spaces, the same study found that the UK’s population ranked lowest in Europe for their oneness with the natural world. This has been linked to the country’s gradual wildlife and nature decline.

Partnering with The Natural History museum, our team was eager to reframe these statistics, and give control back to a population, threatened by environmental decline. By creating a pop-up experience to highlight the relationship between humans and nature, our brief was to interest, educate, and involve an audience in this conversation.

We started by planning a public and accessible experience, in order to reach the widest audience possible, given the universality of the topic. Our initial ideas surrounded the ephemerality of a ‘pop-up’, and we discussed immersive sounds, projections, and temporary structures, to draw people in. We planned to pair this experience with a long-term medium, such as a pamphlet to take home, in order to ensure the longevity of our message.

Through continued group discussion, our idea began evolving around time constraints, scale, budget and realism. Our thinking surrounding sound design shifted from individual headphone experiences, to stereo speaker sound - projected to a large audience at a time, sacrificing a layer of immersion but better suiting and uniting our population. Accompanying our soundscape, we began designing a paper, mixed-media animation, proposing a reality where humans no longer existed and nature reclaimed the urban world; as well as a field guide to navigating sustainable behaviour as a keepsake.

We trialed these amongst our peers, and were met with positive feedback, though we soon realised that we could take our own advice; and be even more sustainable in our execution of our message.

Having already bought seeded paper for our publication design, we had passed the opportunity to create it ourselves; though being recently inspired by our feedback, we revisited and researched this medium and included instructions on how to make plantable paper of your own, in order to promote further sustainability and rectify our oversight. As we encouraged the planting of our publication after its use, we also began digitalising the project, in order to make it permanently accessible as well as paperless.

Despite needing further development to better embody our objectives, through the project creation alone, our group had already better educated ourselves, and our peers, on environmental mindfulness.

Much like our creative process, contributions to sustainability don’t need to be immediately flawless, or even substantial, in order to make a difference. Just by navigating every-day tasks consciously and responsibly, you will contribute to combatting environmental decline and resultant disconnection. If we can reframe the narrative surrounding this conversation from pessimism, to one of a hopeful future; we believe that more people will be encouraged to embrace the natural world.

Through our mixed media film and publication we explore the balance of humanity and nature and how to coexist. Our take-away message from this project is that ‘every act helps’.
